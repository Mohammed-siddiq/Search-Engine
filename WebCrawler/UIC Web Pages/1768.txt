              <h1 class="_title post-title">Submit Your Idea!</h1>
        
      
  
      <p class="_subtitle">Do you have a good idea?</p>
  
  <p>Is your idea:</p>
<ul>
<li>A research tool, a piece of software, a database, or a written curriculum?</li>
<li>Do you have a prototype?</li>
<li>Is it non-obvious, novel and useful?</li>
<li>Have you performed cell studies or animal studies?</li>
</ul>
<p>The OTM staff is experienced in screening innovations for potential commercial use. After the screen, our tech managers will sit down and provide a report of findings and work with our University innovators on a strategy for commercialization. This strategy may involve transferring knowledge and intellectual property rights to an outside entity interested in further developing the innovation, or it may involve helping a lab member create a startup venture based on University research.</p>

    <div class="component-description">
                    <h2 id="planning-ahead" class="__title invisible">Planning ahead?</h2>
                    </div>

  
  <p>Investigators should adequately plan for the possibility of the creation of intellectual property during the course of research using the guidelines below:</p>
<ul>
<li>Keep good laboratory records</li>
<li>Use permanently bound notebooks</li>
<li>Make legible and complete entries</li>
<li>Update in ink.</li>
<li>Record the date and initials/names of the contributor on each page including sketches, photos or other additions.</li>
<li>Use a new page for new experiments.</li>
<li>Use a diagonal line to mark out blank portions of pages.</li>
<li>If you make an error in the entry, draw a single line through with an initial and date. Don’t erase or completely cross it out.</li>
<li>Record all observations, even if the significance is not known.</li>
<li>Record any thoughts on future experiments that should be done.</li>
<li>Other data from equipment should be entered when possible and with handwritten initials and date.</li>
<li>Electronic/computer files, diskettes, and similar data may be useful, but do not carry as much proof as the lab record because they may be easier to alter.</li>
<li>Have the records “witnessed” with signature and date by someone who can understand the science or content. Do not use coworkers, supervisors, or other collaborators in the research. An objective witness is best.</li>
</ul>
<p>Understand the terms and provisions of your sponsored research. Usually, sponsors have the option to license inventions created under the research contract within the time limit of a few months.</p>



  
</div>

  
</section>

    

</div>

      </dd>
      </dl>
